Yiftach Argaman

Postdoctoral researcher · Emotion & Motivation Lab · University of Greifswald

I am a postdoc at the Emotion & Motivation Lab at the University of Greifswald (Psychology). I study social emotions (shame, pride, envy, and regret) and focus on how they shape and are shaped by social context; how they can be regulated to produce context-adaptive outcomes, both personally and socially.

A central question in my work is how social emotions regulate motivations, as well as reputation and status. In previous work I examined shame and pride through their action tendencies: concealment in shame and exposure in pride. In behavioral experimental settings studies, we found that these behaviors change according to the social context and the content of the information exposed. Building on this foundation, with a group of international collaborators from eight nations, we led a large-scale cross-cultural investigation demonstrating that the system of shame functions by monitoring the magnitude and visibility of events. Our two-factor model was replicated across diverse cultural samples differing on key cultural dimensions (e.g., collectivism–individualism, honor–face–dignity), providing evidence that shame is a universal system that acts according to adaptive logic, rather than being primarily a cultural construction or maladaptive as is often argued.

In one current project I study how socioeconomic status shapes envy, focusing on what determines whether envy takes a malicious or benign form. These two types of envy predict different motivations: malicious envy motivates hostility, whereas benign envy drives self-improvement. In a line of eight studies we investigated how the interaction between people's own SES and the SES of envy's target shapes the two types of envy. In another project we study how envy, and specifically the synergy between mechanisms of benign and malicious envy, affects status and well-being. Under the assumption that envy is an adaptation that helps people close status gaps, and based on our previous findings, we predict that the tendency to experience both benign and malicious envy improves status equalization and well-being. In a separate project on envy, we developed a new framework for emotion regulation aimed at transforming envy into its benign form.

Beyond my core work on social emotions, I have conducted basic research on emotion. In one project we used habituation to provide evidence that the IAT measures stable affective knowledge (attitudes) rather than emotional responses. In another, we examined the role of conceptual properties in emotional learning following repeated exposure, showing that abstraction processes of categorization shape affective habituation and that these effects are contingent on valence.

I am also interested in applied research with the aim of pursuing social justice. In a recent work, I reviewed inclusion and exclusion in gaming communities and proposed a model for more equitable gaming.
